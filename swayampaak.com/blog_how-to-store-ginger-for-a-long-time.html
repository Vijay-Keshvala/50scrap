How to Store Ginger for a Long Time? 4 Easy Tips & Tricks

  • Swayampaak Kitchen
  • May 13, 2025

Adrak aka ginger, is needed in everything, Chutney, tempering, Tea and more. Agar kitchen me Adrak nahi hai, to ghar me kalesh hoga [You translate it]. From black tea to regular tea, to masalas and the favourite ginger-garlic paste, it is needed everywhere regularly. For all these purposes, “How to store ginger for a long time?” is the question.

In every Indian household, ginger (adrak) is more than just a kitchen staple — it’s a must-have ingredient for everyday cooking and home remedies. Whether it’s a hot cup of adrak chai on a rainy day or the base of a flavourful curry, ginger adds that special zing to our meals.

But how often do we find it shrivelled, mouldy, or dried out before we get to use it all? Proper storage can make all the difference. In this blog, we’ll explore simple and effective ways of how to store ginger for a long time — keeping it fresh, aromatic, and ready to use, whether you buy it in bulk or harvest it from your own backyard garden.

Understanding Ginger Spoilage

Before diving into storage methods, it’s important to understand why ginger spoils in the first place.

Fresh ginger should be firm to the touch with smooth, unwrinkled skin. The flesh inside should be vibrant yellow with a strong, aromatic smell. When ginger begins to spoil, you’ll notice:

  • Soft, mushy spots
  • Wrinkled, dried-out skin
  • Mould (usually white or green patches)
  • An off smell or diminished aroma
  • Visible dark spots inside when cut open

Several factors accelerate ginger spoilage:

  • Excess moisture: Creates the perfect environment for mould and bacteria growth
  • Warm temperatures: Speed up deterioration
  • Air exposure: Causes oxidation and drying out
  • Light exposure: Degrades quality over time

Proper storage techniques address these factors to maximise shelf life. With the right methods, you can extend ginger’s usability from just a week to several months!

How to Store Ginger for a Long Time?

Ginger is a staple in many kitchens across the world, celebrated not only for its warm, spicy flavour but also for its numerous health benefits. Whether you’re using it to add depth to curries, spice up teas, or for its medicinal properties, fresh ginger can elevate any dish. So, how to store ginger for a long time?

One common issue most people face is ginger going bad too quickly. The good news? With a few simple techniques, you can store ginger for weeks or even months without losing its flavour or nutritional value.

Selecting the Best Ginger for Storage

Your storage success begins at the grocery store or market. Choose ginger with these characteristics:

  • Firm texture: The root should feel solid with no soft spots
  • Smooth skin: Look for tight, unwrinkled skin without blemishes
  • Fresh aroma: When you scratch the skin lightly, it should release a strong, spicy scent
  • Plump pieces: Choose pieces with full “fingers” rather than shrivelled ones
  • Minimal joints: Fewer knobs and joints generally means easier peeling and less waste

For long-term storage, organic ginger is often preferable as conventional ginger may be treated with growth inhibitors or preservatives that affect natural sprouting (which can be beneficial for extending shelf life in some cases).

The best time to buy ginger for storage is when it’s in peak season, typically fall through early spring in most markets. This is when you’ll find the freshest, most robust roots with the highest potential for successful long-term storage.

Short-Term Storage Methods (1-2 weeks)

If you plan to use your ginger within a week or two, these simple storage methods will keep it fresh:

Room Temperature Storage

Storing ginger at room temperature works best if you’ll use it within 5-7 days. This method preserves the strongest flavour but has the shortest shelf life.

How to do it:

  1. Place the unpeeled ginger in a cool, dry spot away from direct sunlight
  2. A kitchen counter away from windows or a pantry shelf works well
  3. Avoid placing it near fruits that produce ethylene gas (like bananas or apples), which can accelerate spoilage
  • Pros: Maintains optimal flavour and is ready to use immediately
  • Cons: Limited shelf life; spoils quickly in warm or humid environments

Refrigerator Storage

Refrigeration extends ginger’s freshness to about 2 weeks with minimal preparation.

How to do it:

  1. Leave the skin on the ginger (it acts as a natural protector)
  2. Place unwashed, unpeeled ginger in a resealable plastic bag
  3. Press out as much air as possible before sealing
  4. Store in the vegetable crisper drawer of your refrigerator

Best containers for short-term refrigeration:

  • Resealable plastic bags with excess air removed
  • Paper bags with a few ventilation holes
  • Reusable produce bags that allow slight airflow

Medium-Term Storage Methods (2-4 weeks)

To keep ginger fresh for up to a month, try these proven techniques:

Refrigeration in Paper Towels

This method manages moisture, the enemy of fresh ginger, effectively.

How to do it:

  1. Wrap unpeeled ginger loosely in paper towels
  2. Place wrapped ginger in a paper bag or an open plastic bag with a few holes
  3. Store in the refrigerator’s crisper drawer
  4. Check weekly and replace damp paper towels as needed

Why it works: The paper towels absorb excess moisture while preventing the ginger from drying out completely.

Crisper Drawer Technique

Your refrigerator’s crisper drawer is designed to maintain ideal humidity for produce.

How to do it:

  1. Adjust the crisper drawer to a low humidity setting (if your refrigerator allows)
  2. Place unpeeled, unwashed ginger directly in the drawer
  3. Keep ginger away from ethylene-producing fruits and vegetables
  4. Store in the front of the drawer where you’ll remember to use it

Submerged in Water

This unusual but effective method can keep ginger fresh for 3-4 weeks.

How to do it:

  1. Place unpeeled ginger in a clean glass jar or container
  2. Fill completely with cold water to cover the ginger
  3. Cover with a lid and place in the refrigerator
  4. Change the water every 2-3 days to prevent bacterial growth

Why it works: The water creates a protective barrier against air while maintaining moisture balance.

Long-Term Storage Methods (1+ months)

For truly extended ginger storage measured in months rather than weeks, these methods are your best options:

Freezing Whole Ginger Root

Freezing preserves ginger for up to 6 months with minimal quality loss.

How to do it:

  1. Clean ginger thoroughly and dry completely
  2. Place whole, unpeeled ginger in a freezer-safe bag or container
  3. Remove as much air as possible from the bag
  4. Label with the date and store in the freezer
  5. When needed, remove from freezer and grate or slice while still frozen (no need to thaw!)

Pro tip: No need to peel before freezing—frozen ginger is actually easier to peel, and the skin grates away easily when using a microplane.

Freezing Grated/Minced Ginger

Pre-processed frozen ginger offers maximum convenience for future use.

How to do it:

  1. Peel and grate or mince ginger
  2. Two options for storage:
    • Option A: Pack into ice cube trays (about 1 tablespoon per compartment), freeze until solid, then transfer cubes to freezer bags
    • Option B: Form into a thin sheet on parchment paper or flatten inside a freezer bag, freeze flat, then break off pieces as needed
  3. Label with date and return to freezer for up to 6 months

Preserving in Alcohol

Alcohol preservation creates flavourful ginger that lasts for months while taking on subtle flavour notes from the alcohol.

How to do it:

  1. Peel and slice ginger thinly
  2. Place in a clean glass jar
  3. Cover completely with vodka, sake, or dry sherry
  4. Seal tightly and store in the refrigerator for up to 3 months
  5. As a bonus, the alcohol becomes infused for use in cooking or cocktails

Best alcohols to use:

  • Vodka (neutral flavour)
  • Dry sherry (adds mild sweetness)
  • Rice wine (complements Asian dishes)

Drying/Dehydrating Techniques

Dried ginger can last for 12+ months and takes up minimal storage space.

How to do it:

Oven method:

  1. Peel ginger and slice very thinly (1/8″ or less)
  2. Arrange in a single layer on a parchment-lined baking sheet
  3. Dry in oven at lowest setting (usually 150-170°F) with door slightly ajar for 1.5-3 hours
  4. Pieces should be completely dry with no moisture remaining

Dehydrator method:

  1. Peel and slice ginger thinly
  2. Arrange on dehydrator trays with space between pieces
  3. Dehydrate at 135°F for 3-5 hours until completely dry and brittle

Air-drying method (humid climates not recommended):

  1. Peel and slice ginger very thinly
  2. String pieces on kitchen twine with space between
  3. Hang in a warm, dry area with good air circulation for 5-7 days

Store dried ginger in airtight containers away from light and heat. Grind as needed for powder, or rehydrate in warm water for recipes.

Pickling Methods

Pickled ginger offers a tangy flavour and an extended shelf life of 3-6 months.

Simple pickling recipe:

  1. Peel and slice ginger very thinly (a mandoline works best)
  2. Sprinkle with 1 teaspoon salt and let sit for 30 minutes
  3. Rinse and pat dry
  4. Bring 1 cup rice vinegar, 1/2 cup sugar, and 1 teaspoon salt to a boil
  5. Pour hot liquid over ginger in a sterilised jar
  6. Cool completely before sealing and refrigerating

For pink pickled ginger (like served with sushi), use very young ginger, which naturally turns pink when pickled. Alternatively, add a small slice of beet to the pickling liquid.

Advanced Preservation Techniques

For ginger enthusiasts, these specialised methods offer unique flavours and extended storage:

Fermenting Ginger

Fermentation creates probiotic-rich ginger with complex flavour and a 2-3 month shelf life.

How to ferment ginger:

  1. Peel and chop ginger into small pieces
  2. Add to a clean glass jar, filling 3/4 full
  3. Prepare a 2% salt brine (about 1 tablespoon salt per quart of water)
  4. Pour brine over ginger, ensuring it’s completely submerged
  5. Cover with a fermentation weight and an airlock lid
  6. Ferment at room temperature for 1-2 weeks, checking daily
  7. Once the desired tanginess is achieved, transfer to the refrigerator

Fermented ginger is excellent in dressings, marinades, and beverages.

Candying Ginger

Candied ginger lasts for 6+ months and serves as both a preservation method and a delicious treat.

Basic recipe:

  1. Peel and slice ginger into 1/8″ pieces
  2. Place in a saucepan and cover with water
  3. Bring to a boil, then simmer for 30 minutes until tender
  4. Drain, reserving 1/4 cup cooking liquid
  5. Weigh the ginger and measure an equal amount of sugar
  6. Return ginger to the pan with sugar and reserved liquid
  7. Simmer gently, stirring occasionally, until the syrup thickens (30-40 minutes)
  8. Transfer pieces to a wire rack over parchment paper to dry (8-12 hours)
  9. Toss in additional sugar if desired
  10. Store in an airtight container in a cool, dry place

Making Ginger Syrup or Paste

Processed ginger concentrates are versatile for cooking and last 3-4 months refrigerated.

Ginger syrup:

  1. Combine 1 cup chopped ginger, 1 cup sugar, and 1 cup water in a saucepan
  2. Bring to a boil, then reduce heat and simmer for 45 minutes
  3. Strain through a fine mesh sieve
  4. Cool completely and transfer to a sterile bottle
  5. Store refrigerated for up to 3 months

Ginger paste:

  1. Peel and roughly chop ginger
  2. Process in a food processor until finely minced
  3. Add 1 tablespoon neutral oil per cup of ginger
  4. Process until a smooth paste forms
  5. Transfer to a clean jar, topped with a thin layer of oil
  6. Store refrigerated for up to 3 months

Reviving and Using Stored Ginger

Different storage methods require different approaches when it’s time to use your preserved ginger:

How to Thaw Frozen Ginger

The beauty of frozen ginger is that it typically doesn’t require thawing:

  • For grated or diced needs: Grate frozen ginger directly with a microplane grater—it’s actually easier than grating fresh!
  • For slices: Remove from freezer about 5 minutes before cutting to slightly soften
  • For stir-fries or dishes where texture isn’t critical: Use directly from frozen
  • For infusions (like tea): Add frozen pieces directly to hot water

If you must thaw completely, place in the refrigerator overnight rather than using room temperature to prevent texture degradation.

Using Dried Ginger

Dried ginger has a more concentrated flavour than fresh, requiring adjustment in recipes:

  • For powdered ginger: Grind dried pieces in a spice grinder as needed
  • Substitute ratio: 1 tablespoon fresh ginger = 1/4 teaspoon dried ground ginger
  • To rehydrate: Soak in warm water for 1-2 hours before using in recipes where texture matters
  • For teas and broths: Add dried pieces directly to simmering liquid and strain before serving

Incorporating Preserved Ginger in Recipes

Different preservation methods create different flavour profiles to enhance various dishes:

  • Alcohol-preserved ginger: Ideal for stir-fries, marinades, and sauces where the alcohol cooks off; the infused alcohol makes excellent cocktails or deglazing liquid
  • Pickled ginger: Perfect as a condiment for Asian dishes, chopped in salad dressings, or added to grain bowls
  • Fermented ginger: Adds probiotic benefits and complex flavour to smoothies, dressings, and composed salads
  • Candied ginger: Use in baking, chop for topping desserts, or enjoy as a sweet-spicy snack
  • Ginger syrup: Perfect for beverages, cocktails, glazes, and desserts
  • Ginger paste: The ultimate convenience for curries, marinades, dressings, and any recipe calling for fresh ginger

Troubleshooting Common Storage Problems

Even with proper storage, you might encounter these common issues:

Dealing with Mouldy Ginger

If you discover mould on stored ginger:

  • For small affected areas: Cut away the mouldy portion plus 1/2 inch around it; check the remaining ginger carefully before using
  • For extensive mould: Unfortunately, it’s best to discard the entire piece to avoid the risk of invisible mould spread
  • Prevention: Ensure ginger is completely dry before storing, check regularly during storage, and maintain proper airflow

Preventing Freezer Burn

Freezer burn affects the quality and flavour of frozen ginger:

  • Double-wrap frozen ginger or use vacuum-sealed bags
  • Use frozen ginger within 6 months for the best quality
  • Store in the deepest part of the freezer where the temperature remains most consistent
  • If freezer burn occurs, trim affected areas before using

Solutions for Sprouting Ginger

Finding sprouts on your ginger isn’t necessarily bad:

  • Sprouted ginger is still safe to eat—simply cut away the sprouts if desired
  • For intentional sprouting (extending storage life): Place ginger in soil with buds facing up, cover lightly with soil, keep moist in a bright spot
  • Sprouted ginger will eventually grow new rhizomes, you can harvest
  • Sprouted ginger tends to be more bitter, so best used in cooked applications rather than raw

Conclusion

With so many effective storage options, you never need to waste ginger again! Choose your preservation method based on how long you need it to last:

  • Short-term (days to a week): Room temperature storage
  • Medium-term (2-4 weeks): Refrigeration with paper towels or water submersion
  • Long-term (1-6+ months): Freezing, drying, pickling, or alcohol preservation
  • Extended preservation with processing (3-12+ months): Fermenting, candying, or creating syrups and pastes

Remember that proper storage begins with selecting quality ginger and handling it appropriately from the moment you bring it home. By implementing these techniques, you’ll always have flavourful ginger ready for your culinary adventures!
